The Basic Characters

Katakana consists of 5 base vowels, 42 consonant-vowel pairs, and 1 consonant-only character. Learning the base vowels is the best place to start.

Vowels

While English has 14 to 21 to vowel sounds depending on the dialect, Japanese only uses five vowel sounds. It's important to remember that an 'a' for example is always pronounced as ah regardless of what words are around it.

Katakana ア イ ウ エ オ
Romaji a i u e o
Sound rod read rude red road

It's worth noting that some Japanese words are mispronounced in English. For example, karaoke often ends with an ee sound in English when it should end with an eh sound. The same thing also happens with sake and karate. Nothing affects the vowel's sound. There's no emphasizing syllables in Japanese.

Vowels + Consonants

The other katakana characters are created by simply adding a consonant in front of each vowel sound. Again, each character represents one syllable.

a i u e o
k カ ka キ ki ク ku ケ ke コ ko
s サsa シshi スsu セse ソso
t タ ta チ chi ツ tsu テ te ト to
n ナ na ニ ni ヌ nu ネ ne ノ no
h ハ ha ヒ hi フ fu ヘ he ホ ho
m マ ma ミ mi ム mu メ me モ mo
y ヤ ya ユ yu ヨ yo
r/l ラ ra リ ri ル ru レ re ロ ro
w ワ wa ヲ wo

Remember the vowel sounds don’t change. Don’t apply English phonics rules to romaji

Exceptions

A few characters deviate slightly from the established pattern. In romaji, they can either be written the way they sound or the way that matches consonant vowel pattern. While there are two ways to write these syllables in romaji, they only have one sound

Katakana Romaji Sound
チ chi / ti cheap
シ shi / si sheep
ツ tsu / tu wetsuit

Some other characters can be spelled one of two ways in romaji because the Japanese sound is actually somewhere in between.

Katakana Romaji Sound
ラ ra / la rla
リ ri / li rli
ル ru / lu rlu
レ re / le rle
ロ ro / lo rlo
フ fu / hu fhu

It can be very difficult for English-speakers to pronounce these characters correctly.

For this reading lesson, just know that these characters can repersent one of two English sounds.

ライト right light
フード food hood

When reading katakana, check both possible sounds of these characters. One might make more sense than the other.

'n' and 'm'

There’s one consonant-only syllable in Japanese. However, it can be one of two sounds depending on the word.

ン n/m

If you're interested in speaking Japanese, this character is treated as a full syllable even though it is only one letter in English.
