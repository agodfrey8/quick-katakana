Potential Problems

While katakana doesn’t require learning Japanese vocabulary, it isn't always easy to read. Sometimes reading katakana can feel like solving a puzzle when it isn’t immediately clear what English word is being used. There are a few reasons this can happen.

Character Confusion

The following characters are very easy to confuse with eachother. Especially when they are printed.

ン n ソ so
シ shi ツ tsu

Keep these characters in mind while trying to read katakana. If a katakana word isn’t matching an English word, consider changing a character for a similar looking character and trying again.

Phonetic Limitations

The limitations of the Japanese phonetics can create strange versions of English words. Sometimes borrowed words have a few extra sounds in them because of the syllables available.

  • ツナ – tsuna – tuna
  • シーディー – shīdī – CD

This is especially true when a borrowed word ends in a consonant other the ‘n’ or ‘m.’ Japanese adds an extra vowel sound to the end of these words.

  • カード – kādo – card
  • スポーツ – supōtsu – sports

Sometimes the limited vowel sounds of Japanese will cause a borrowed word to sound like another English word.

  • カット – katto – (hair)cut (NOT cat)

Long English words become very complicated in Japanese and can become almost impossible to link back to the original. This is especially true for words with consonant clusters since Japanese syllables are consonant-vowel pairs.

  • マクドナルド – Makudonarudo – McDonald’s
  • エキストラ – ekisutora – extra

Cultural Differences

Often, an English word will evolve over time and have its own cultural meaning in Japan.

For example, Japan sometimes shortens English words in ways unfamiliar to native English-speakers. Some extra thought and context clues might be enough to make the connection.

  • アパート – apaato – apart(ment)
  • エアコン – eacon – air con(ditioner)
  • パソコン – pasocon – perso(nal) com(puter)

However, sometimes Japan uses parts of English words that overlap with a other words. Situational context is even more important here.

  • サンド – sando – sand(wich)
  • アイス – aisu – ice (cream)

Other times Japan uses English words in a completely original way. At first, these are almost impossible to understand even with context, and require some exposure to Japanese culture to get.

  • アメリカンドッグ – amerikandoggu – American dog (a corn dog)
  • ファイト – faito – fight (“Do your best!”)
  • バイキング – baikingu – viking (buffet)
  • Sometimes written: ヴァイキング – vaikingu

In a few cases, katakana words are borrowed from other languages. Like katakana words that are unique to Japan, these words just take time to learn through experience in Japan.

  • アルバイト – aurbaito – (part-time) work – German
  • パン – pan – bread – Spanish/French
  • ラーメン – ramen – Chinese
