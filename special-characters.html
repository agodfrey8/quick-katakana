Special Characters

Memorizing the base characters is biggest part of learning katakana. This game is a great way to practice, but it requires knowing how base character can be modified first. The following characters simply modify the base characters to make slightly different syllables.

Dots and Circles

Two dots above the top right corner of a character are the most common way a base character is modified. Dots change the consonant sound of a syllable into another similar consonant sound. For example, a k sound to a g sound or a s sound to a z sound. A circle above a character does the same thing. However, only 'h' characters use the circle modification. It modifies a h sound into a p sound.

Base Dots Circle
K – カ ka G – ガ ga
S – サ sa Z – ザ za
T – タ ta D – ダ da
H – ハ ha B – バ ba P – パ pa

The same three characters that had different pronounciation rules before have different rules here as well.

Katakana Romaji Sound
ヂ “ji” or “di” jeep
ジ “ji” jeep
ヅ “dzu” or “du” or ”zu” zoo

ヂ and ヅ are rarely ever used though.

There is a special use of dots that is unique to katakana.

ウ – u ヴ – v

However, the sound is more like vw because the v sound is difficult for most Japanese speakers. Instead, it is much more common to use b sounds in place of v sounds.

  • ビデオ – bideo – video
  • ビタミン – bitamin – vitamin

Small Characters

Sometimes, characters will appear that are half the size of the other characters around them. This is because the characters aren't syllables themselves, but are modifying the syllables around them. Small ヤ, ユ, ヨ, and ツ appear the most frequently.

When a syllable ends with an i sound and is followed by a small ヤ, ユ, or ヨ a whole new syllable is created.

Base Two Syllables One Syllable
キ – ki キヤ – kiya キャ – kya キュ – kyu キョ – kyo
ギ – gi ギヤ – giya ギャ – gya ギュ – gyu ギョ – gyo

The same applies for ニ, ヒ, ミ, リ, ビ, ピ

  • キャベツ – kyabeji – cabbage
  • リニューアル – rinyūaru – renewal

However, like before, there are slightly different rules for シ, ジ, チ

シ – shi シャ – sha シュ – shu ショ – sho
ジ – ji ジャ – ja ジュ – ju ジョ – jo
チ – chi チャ – cha チュ – chu チョ – cho
  • カシューナッツ – kashūnattsu – cashew nuts
  • ジャンプ – janpu – jump

Sometimes characters such as テ, フ, チ, and others will have a small vowel character next to them. This is unique to katakana. By combining the consonant sound of the big character with the vowel sound of the small one, a new syllable is created that isn’t native to Japanese.

  • ティーンエージャー – tīneijā – teenager
  • ファイター – faitā – fighter
  • チェリー – cherī – cherry

Lines

A line character after a syllable means to double the vowel sound. In romaji, a line is usually written over the elongated vowel instead of writing the vowel twice.

  • カレー – karee/karē – curry
  • プレーヤー – pureeyaa/purēyā – player

Remember, nothing changes the sound of a vowel in Japanese. This is not a long vowel like in English. The sound stays the same, but the duration is extended.

Small "tsu"

A small ツ character before a syllable means to double the consonant sound. In romaji, the elongated consonant is written twice.

  • バッグ – baggu – (computer) bug
  • Compared to: バグ – bagu – bag
  • カット – katto – (hair)cut

To double a consonant, hold your tongue in place for a moment before releasing it. This is difficult for English speakers since long consonant sounds don’t exist in English.

Spaces

Circles between words represent spaces if they are necessary. These are rare because Japanese doesn’t normally use spaces, but are sometimes used for foreign names and titles.

  • ジョージ・クルーニー – jōji kurūnī – George Clooney
  • ダイ・ハード – dai hādo – Die Hard
